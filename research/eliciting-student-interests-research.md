# Research Report: Eliciting Student Interests, Strengths & Passions

**Date**: March 2, 2026
**Purpose**: Deep research on frameworks, techniques, and questions for discovering what a high schooler cares about, is good at, and could pursue

---

## 1. Established Frameworks for Interest Discovery

### Holland Code (RIASEC) Model

The [Holland Code framework](https://www.careerkey.org/fit/personality/holland-code-assessment-riasec) is the most widely-used career interest taxonomy, developed by psychologist John L. Holland. It categorizes people into six personality/interest types:

| Code | Type | Description | Example Activities |
|------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **R** | Realistic | Hands-on, practical, physical | Building, repairing, outdoor work, athletics |
| **I** | Investigative | Analytical, intellectual, curious | Research, science, math, problem-solving |
| **A** | Artistic | Creative, expressive, original | Writing, music, art, design, performing |
| **S** | Social | Helping, teaching, nurturing | Counseling, teaching, volunteering, mentoring |
| **E** | Enterprising | Leading, persuading, managing | Business, debate, student government, sales |
| **C** | Conventional | Organizing, detail-oriented, systematic | Data, finance, planning, administration |

**How to use it**: Most people have 2-3 dominant types. Matching career environments to personality types leads to higher satisfaction and performance. The [O*NET Interest Profiler](https://www.careeronestop.org/ExploreCareers/Assessments/interests.aspx) (free, from US Dept of Labor) is the gold standard online assessment.

**For the AI skill**: Walk the student through scenarios in each category and gauge their energy/enthusiasm level to build an informal RIASEC profile.

### Ikigai Framework

The [Japanese concept of Ikigai](https://imagine.jhu.edu/resources/ikigai/) ("reason for being") is a powerful framework for [helping students find purpose](https://cwi.pressbooks.pub/pathwaystocollegesuccess/chapter/discovering-your-ikigai-finding-purpose-in-college-and-career/). It maps the intersection of four circles:

```
            What You LOVE
                 |
     Passion --- | --- Mission
         |       |       |
  What You're  --+--  What the
  GOOD AT        |    World NEEDS
         |       |       |
    Profession - | - Vocation
                 |
          What You Can
          Be PAID FOR
```

**Key intersections**:
- **Passion** = What you love + What you're good at
- **Mission** = What you love + What the world needs
- **Profession** = What you're good at + What you can be paid for
- **Vocation** = What the world needs + What you can be paid for
- **Ikigai** = The center where ALL four overlap

**For the AI skill**: Use this as a structured exploration framework. Walk through each circle with dedicated questions, then help the student see where they overlap.

### Gardner's Multiple Intelligences

[Gardner's theory](https://www.niu.edu/citl/resources/guides/instructional-guide/gardners-theory-of-multiple-intelligences.shtml) identifies nine types of intelligence, helping students recognize strengths beyond traditional academic measures:

| Intelligence | Description | Indicators |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Linguistic** | Word-smart | Loves reading, writing, storytelling, word games |
| **Logical-Mathematical** | Number/reasoning-smart | Enjoys puzzles, patterns, experiments, strategy games |
| **Spatial** | Picture-smart | Thinks in images, good with maps, design, visualization |
| **Musical** | Music-smart | Sensitive to rhythm, pitch, melody; plays instruments |
| **Bodily-Kinesthetic** | Body-smart | Learns by doing, excels at sports, crafts, hands-on work |
| **Interpersonal** | People-smart | Reads others well, natural leader, thrives in groups |
| **Intrapersonal** | Self-smart | Self-aware, reflective, independent, knows own motivations |
| **Naturalistic** | Nature-smart | Drawn to nature, animals, environmental patterns |
| **Existential** | Life-smart | Asks deep questions about meaning, purpose, existence |

**For the AI skill**: Use as a lens to broaden the student's self-concept. Many students who struggle academically have strong kinesthetic, musical, or interpersonal intelligence that gets overlooked.

### VIA Character Strengths

The [VIA (Values in Action) framework](https://www.viacharacter.org/character-strengths) identifies 24 character strengths organized under six virtues. [Research shows](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12057798/) these correlate with life satisfaction, achievement, and well-being in adolescents:

**Six Virtue Categories**:
1. **Wisdom**: Creativity, curiosity, judgment, love of learning, perspective
2. **Courage**: Bravery, perseverance, honesty, zest
3. **Humanity**: Love, kindness, social intelligence
4. **Justice**: Teamwork, fairness, leadership
5. **Temperance**: Forgiveness, humility, prudence, self-regulation
6. **Transcendence**: Appreciation of beauty, gratitude, hope, humor, spirituality

**Key insight**: Character strengths are distinct from talents. Talents are more genetically influenced (athletic ability, musical ear), while character strengths (perseverance, kindness, curiosity) are developable.

**For the AI skill**: Help students identify their "signature strengths" (top 3-5) and explore how those strengths could apply to different academic and career paths. A strength-based approach [shifts the narrative from "what's wrong" to "what's strong"](https://www.impact-psych.com/blog/building-strength-based-approaches-for-adolescent-clients).

### Flow Theory (Csikszentmihalyi)

[Flow](https://www.pursuit-of-happiness.org/history-of-happiness/mihaly-csikszentmihalyi/) is the state of being completely immersed in an activity with intense focus and creative engagement. It's one of the strongest signals of genuine interest.

**Conditions for flow**:
1. Clear goals and sense of progress
2. Clear and immediate feedback
3. Balance between challenge level and skill level (not too easy, not too hard)

**Teen-specific findings**:
- Teens experience flow 13% of the time watching TV, 34% doing hobbies, and **44% in sports and games**
- [High-flow teens](https://www.pursuit-of-happiness.org/science-of-happiness/getting-in-the-flow/) spend more time on hobbies, sports, and homework
- High-flow teens have **greater long-term happiness AND success** in school, relationships, and careers
- Flow must start with something that fits passions, interests, or internal drive

**For the AI skill**: The #1 question for identifying interests is essentially a flow-detection question: "What activities make you lose track of time?" Follow up with: "What was it about that activity that made it so engaging?"

## 2. Elicitation Techniques

### Motivational Interviewing Questions for Values & Goals

[MI is especially effective with teens](https://nexusteenacademy.com/motivational-interviewing-questions-for-teens/) because it respects their autonomy. At least **70% of questions should be open-ended**. Key categories:

**Values Exploration**:
- "What qualities come to mind when you think about the kind of person you want to become?"
- "Tell me what is most important in your life at this moment?"
- "Tell me about the things you value and are a priority?"
- "If you could change one thing about the world, what would it be?"

**Goals & Future**:
- "What are some of the things you wish to move toward in your life?"
- "When you think about the future, what are some things you would like to have in it?"
- "Where do you see yourself in 5 years? What does a typical day look like?"

**Discrepancy (connecting values to action)**:
- "How does your current behavior fit within your values?"
- "How does your current behavior support your future goals?"
- "Tell me about the times you are not living out your values as fully as you would like?"

**Important MI principles**:
- Never argue or lecture
- Express empathy and genuine curiosity
- Roll with resistance ("I don't know" is valid information, not a failure)
- Support self-efficacy ("What strengths do you have that could help here?")

### The Values Card Sort

The [Values Card Sort](https://capd.mit.edu/resources/values-card-sort/) is a well-established counseling exercise:

**How it works**:
1. Present a list of values (e.g., achievement, adventure, autonomy, creativity, family, friendship, health, justice, knowledge, leadership, security, service)
2. Student sorts into three piles: **Very Important**, **Somewhat Important**, **Not Important**
3. From "Very Important," narrow down to top 5
4. Discuss: "Why these? What would life look like without this value?"
5. Connect values to potential career paths and activities

**For the AI skill**: Present values conversationally (groups of 3-4 at a time) and have the student rank them. Build a values profile over the conversation.

[Therapist Aid offers teen-specific values worksheets](https://www.therapistaid.com/therapy-worksheets/values/adolescents) that can inform the approach.

### Passion Discovery Questions

Curated from [multiple research sources](https://happiful.com/30-questions-to-help-young-people-discover-their-passions) and [counseling frameworks](https://modernparentingsolutions.org/interests/):

**Flow & Engagement Detectors**:
- "What could you talk about for hours without getting bored?"
- "What activities make you lose track of time?"
- "Describe your perfect Saturday - what would you do if you had zero obligations?"
- "What's something you do that feels more like play than work?"
- "When do you feel most like yourself?"

**Strengths Identifiers**:
- "What do friends come to you for help with?"
- "What subjects or skills come easiest to you?"
- "What have people complimented you on?"
- "If your class had to pick you for something, what would it be?"
- "What's something you can do that most of your friends can't?"

**Values & Purpose Probes**:
- "What problems in the world bother you the most?"
- "If you had a million dollars and didn't need to work, what would you spend your time doing?"
- "Who do you admire most, and what is it about them you admire?"
- "What kind of impact do you want to have on people around you?"
- "What does success mean to you - not your parents or teachers, but YOU?"

**Exploration Prompts**:
- "What's something you've always wanted to try but haven't yet?"
- "If you could shadow any person at their job for a week, who would it be?"
- "What YouTube channels, podcasts, or social media accounts do you follow the most?"
- "What would your dream school project look like?"
- "If you could create a club at school that doesn't exist yet, what would it be about?"

**Important mindset note**: [If a teen shrugs or says "I don't know"](https://www.theyarethefuture.co.uk/how-to-help-your-teenager-find-their-passion/), treat that as information itself. Many teens have never been asked these questions. It's normal. Don't push - plant the seed and return to it.

## 3. Experience-Based Discovery Methods

Beyond questions, real-world experience is essential for interest discovery:

### Active Exploration Strategies
- **Elective courses**: Trying diverse subjects (art, coding, shop, debate)
- **Volunteering**: Exposure to different work environments and causes
- **Job shadowing**: Seeing what careers actually look like day-to-day
- **Clubs and extracurriculars**: Testing group dynamics and leadership
- **Summer programs**: Intensive exposure to specific fields
- **Personal projects**: Building, creating, or organizing something independently
- **Informational interviews**: Talking to adults in interesting careers

### The "Experiment" Mindset
Rather than asking "What's your passion?" (which feels high-stakes), reframe as:
- "What's something interesting you could try this month?"
- "Let's run a 2-week experiment - try X and see how it feels"
- "Your interests at 15 don't have to be your interests at 25. What's interesting NOW?"

This reduces the pressure of "finding your calling" and normalizes exploration.

## 5. Recommended AI Counselor Approach for Interest Elicitation

### Phase 1: Warm-Up & Rapport (Low Stakes)
Start with easy, non-threatening questions to build comfort:
- "What's something fun you did recently?"
- "What's your favorite class right now? What do you like about it?"
- "What do you usually do when you have free time?"

### Phase 2: Flow Detection (Find the Energy)
Listen for signs of genuine engagement - voice changes, more detailed answers, enthusiasm:
- "What could you talk about for hours?"
- "When do you lose track of time?"
- "What's something you chose to learn on your own, not for school?"

### Phase 3: Strengths Mapping (What Are You Good At?)
Help them see their abilities through multiple lenses (not just grades):
- "What do friends or family say you're good at?"
- "What comes naturally to you that others seem to struggle with?"
- "Think about a time you felt really proud of something you did. What was it?"

### Phase 4: Values Clarification (What Matters to You?)
Connect interests to deeper motivations:
- "What kind of problems do you wish someone would solve?"
- "What does a good life look like to you?"
- Present conversational values sort (small groups of contrasting values)

### Phase 5: Possibility Expansion (Broaden the Horizon)
Connect their profile to paths they may not have considered:
- "Based on what you've told me, here are some areas that might interest you..."
- "Have you ever considered X? It combines [their interest] with [their strength]"
- "People with similar interests often enjoy... - does any of that resonate?"

### Phase 6: Action Planning (Make It Concrete)
Turn exploration into experimentation:
- "What's one thing you could try this week to explore that interest?"
- "Would you be interested in trying [specific activity]?"
- "Let's check back in and see how that experiment went"

### Key Principles Throughout
1. **No pressure to "find your passion"** - frame as exploration and experimentation
2. **"I don't know" is valid** - plant seeds, return later
3. **Interests evolve** - what you like at 15 may change at 18 and that's normal
4. **Multiple interests are okay** - you don't need just one thing
5. **Listen for energy, not words** - enthusiasm in voice/detail is more telling than stated preferences
6. **Connect the dots** - help them see patterns across their interests they may not see themselves
