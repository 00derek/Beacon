# elicitation-frameworks — Interest Discovery Frameworks and Question Banks

This module provides the theoretical frameworks, practical questions, and conversation techniques for discovering a student's interests, strengths, values, and potential academic/career directions. It is the backbone of the `discover` command and supports `kickoff` during initial interest exploration.

---

## Framework Summaries

### Holland Code (RIASEC)

The most widely-used career interest taxonomy, developed by psychologist John L. Holland. It categorizes people into six personality/interest types. Most people have 2-3 dominant types.

| Code | Type | Description | Example Activities | Possible Directions |
|------|------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **R** | Realistic | Hands-on, practical, physical | Building, repairing, outdoor work, athletics, cooking | Engineering, trades, agriculture, athletics, military |
| **I** | Investigative | Analytical, intellectual, curious | Research, science, math, puzzles, problem-solving | Science, medicine, technology, academia, data analysis |
| **A** | Artistic | Creative, expressive, original | Writing, music, art, design, performing, filmmaking | Fine arts, design, writing, media, architecture |
| **S** | Social | Helping, teaching, nurturing | Counseling, teaching, volunteering, mentoring, coaching | Education, healthcare, social work, psychology, nonprofit |
| **E** | Enterprising | Leading, persuading, managing | Business, debate, student government, sales, organizing | Business, law, politics, entrepreneurship, management |
| **C** | Conventional | Organizing, detail-oriented, systematic | Data, finance, planning, administration, spreadsheets | Accounting, IT, logistics, administration, banking |

**How to use with a student:**

Walk through scenarios in each category and gauge their energy level. Don't present it as a formal test — weave it into conversation.

- "If you had to pick between building something with your hands, solving a puzzle, or organizing a team — which sounds most appealing?"
- "When you work on group projects, do you usually end up leading, creating, researching, or organizing?"
- "Which of these sounds like the worst job to you?" (Aversions are as informative as preferences.)

**Key insight:** The RIASEC profile is a starting point, not a verdict. Use it to open conversations about what kinds of work environments and activities energize the student.

**Free assessment:** [O*NET Interest Profiler](https://www.careeronestop.org/ExploreCareers/Assessments/interests.aspx) — the gold standard online RIASEC assessment, free from the US Department of Labor.

### Ikigai Framework

The Japanese concept of Ikigai ("reason for being") maps the intersection of four circles to help students find purpose.

```
            What You LOVE
                 |
     Passion --- | --- Mission
         |       |       |
  What You're  --+--  What the
  GOOD AT        |    World NEEDS
         |       |       |
    Profession - | - Vocation
                 |
          What You Can
          Be PAID FOR
```

**Key intersections:**
- **Passion** = What you love + What you're good at
- **Mission** = What you love + What the world needs
- **Profession** = What you're good at + What you can be paid for
- **Vocation** = What the world needs + What you can be paid for
- **Ikigai** = The center where all four overlap

**How to use with a student:**

Walk through each circle with dedicated questions. Most high schoolers will have strong responses to "love" and "good at" but less clarity on "world needs" and "paid for." That's normal — the point is to start the exploration, not finish it.

- **Love:** "What could you do for hours and not get tired of it?"
- **Good at:** "What do people come to you for help with?"
- **World needs:** "What problems in the world bother you the most?"
- **Paid for:** "Do you know anyone who gets paid to do something you think is interesting?"

Then look for overlaps: "It sounds like you love working with animals and you're good at science. Have you ever thought about veterinary medicine, wildlife biology, or animal behavior research?"

**Key insight for teens:** The "paid for" circle can feel intimidating or abstract. Frame it as "what kinds of work exist in this space" rather than "how will you make money." Teens are discovering interests, not choosing a career.

**Free assessment:** [IkigaiTest.com](https://ikigaitest.com/) — guided Ikigai exploration.

### Gardner's Multiple Intelligences

Gardner's theory identifies nine types of intelligence, helping students see their strengths beyond grades and test scores. This is especially valuable for students who feel "not smart" because their strengths lie outside traditional academics.

| Intelligence | Description | Indicators | Career Connections |
|-------------|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| **Linguistic** | Word-smart | Loves reading, writing, storytelling, word games | Writing, journalism, law, teaching, marketing |
| **Logical-Mathematical** | Number/reasoning-smart | Enjoys puzzles, patterns, experiments, strategy games | Science, engineering, finance, programming, research |
| **Spatial** | Picture-smart | Thinks in images, good with maps, design, visualization | Architecture, art, engineering, surgery, film |
| **Musical** | Music-smart | Sensitive to rhythm, pitch, melody; plays instruments | Music, audio engineering, therapy, teaching |
| **Bodily-Kinesthetic** | Body-smart | Learns by doing, excels at sports, crafts, hands-on work | Athletics, trades, surgery, dance, physical therapy |
| **Interpersonal** | People-smart | Reads others well, natural leader, thrives in groups | Management, counseling, sales, teaching, politics |
| **Intrapersonal** | Self-smart | Self-aware, reflective, independent, knows own motivations | Psychology, writing, philosophy, entrepreneurship |
| **Naturalistic** | Nature-smart | Drawn to nature, animals, environmental patterns | Environmental science, veterinary, agriculture, forestry |
| **Existential** | Life-smart | Asks deep questions about meaning, purpose, existence | Philosophy, theology, counseling, social justice, writing |

**How to use with a student:**

Use this as a lens to broaden the student's self-concept. Don't present all nine at once — listen to the student and reflect back which intelligences seem strongest.

- "You mentioned you're always the one friends come to for advice — that's interpersonal intelligence. Have you thought about fields that use that skill?"
- "You said you're not great at school but you're really good with your hands and you love fixing things. That's bodily-kinesthetic intelligence, and it's a real strength. There are entire career paths built around that."

**Key insight:** Many students who struggle academically have strong kinesthetic, musical, or interpersonal intelligence that traditional schooling overlooks. Naming these as legitimate forms of intelligence can be profoundly validating.

### VIA Character Strengths

The VIA (Values in Action) framework identifies 24 character strengths organized under six virtues. Research shows these correlate with life satisfaction, achievement, and well-being in adolescents. Character strengths are distinct from talents — they are developable through practice.

**Six Virtue Categories:**

| Virtue | Strengths |
|--------|-----------|
| **Wisdom** | Creativity, curiosity, judgment, love of learning, perspective |
| **Courage** | Bravery, perseverance, honesty, zest |
| **Humanity** | Love, kindness, social intelligence |
| **Justice** | Teamwork, fairness, leadership |
| **Temperance** | Forgiveness, humility, prudence, self-regulation |
| **Transcendence** | Appreciation of beauty, gratitude, hope, humor, spirituality |

**How to use with a student:**

Help them identify their "signature strengths" (top 3-5) and explore how those strengths connect to academic and career paths.

- "When do you feel most like the best version of yourself? What strengths are you using in those moments?"
- "Your friends would probably say you're [kind / funny / persistent / creative] — does that feel right?"
- "Think about a time you were really proud of something you did. Which of these strengths were you using?"

**Key insight:** A strength-based approach shifts the conversation from "what's wrong with me" to "what's strong about me." This is especially powerful for students with low academic confidence.

**Free assessment:** [VIA Youth Survey](https://www.viacharacter.org/character-strengths) — 96-question assessment for ages 10-17. Takes about 15 minutes.

### Flow Theory (Csikszentmihalyi)

Flow is the state of being completely immersed in an activity with intense focus and creative engagement. It is one of the strongest signals of genuine interest — stronger than stated preferences.

**Conditions for flow:**
1. Clear goals and sense of progress
2. Clear and immediate feedback
3. Balance between challenge level and skill level (not too easy, not too hard)

**Teen-specific findings:**
- Teens experience flow 13% of the time watching TV, 34% doing hobbies, and 44% in sports and games
- High-flow teens spend more time on hobbies, sports, and homework
- High-flow teens have greater long-term happiness AND success in school, relationships, and careers
- Flow must start with something that fits passions, interests, or internal drive

**How to use with a student:**

The best interest-discovery question is essentially a flow-detection question: "What activities make you lose track of time?" Follow up with questions that unpack what specifically creates the flow state:

- "What was it about that activity that made it so engaging?"
- "Was it the challenge? The creativity? Working with others? The feeling of getting better?"
- "When you're doing [activity], does the world kind of disappear?"

**Key insight:** Listen for what the student's body and energy tell you, not just their words. A student who says "I guess I like math" in a flat tone is different from one who says "I love figuring out how things work" with their whole body leaning in. Flow detection is energy detection.

## Six-Phase Elicitation Approach

This is the recommended structure for a full interest discovery conversation. Phases are sequential but don't need to happen in one session. The counselor can revisit earlier phases as needed.

### Phase 1: Warm-Up and Rapport (Low Stakes)

**Purpose:** Build comfort. Reduce pressure. Signal that there are no wrong answers.

**Questions:**
- "What's something fun you did recently?"
- "What's your favorite class right now? What do you like about it?"
- "What do you usually do when you have free time?"
- "What's something you've been into lately — could be anything, doesn't have to be school-related?"

**What to listen for:**
- How much detail they offer (detail = engagement)
- What they volunteer vs. what they need to be asked about
- Whether they relax or stay guarded

**Transition to Phase 2:** When the student is talking freely and seems comfortable, shift to deeper engagement questions.

---

### Phase 2: Flow Detection (Find the Energy)

**Purpose:** Identify activities where the student experiences genuine engagement — not just stated preferences, but felt experience.

**Questions:**
- "What could you talk about for hours without getting bored?"
- "What activities make you lose track of time?"
- "What's something you chose to learn on your own — not for school, just because you wanted to?"
- "Describe your perfect Saturday — what would you do if you had zero obligations?"
- "What's something you do that feels more like play than work?"

**What to listen for:**
- Energy changes: voice gets faster, more animated, more detailed
- Specific examples vs. vague generalities (specificity = real engagement)
- The difference between "I like" (mild) and "I love" / "I can't stop" (flow)
- Activities they return to voluntarily, not because someone told them to

**Transition to Phase 3:** When you've identified 1-3 activities that seem to genuinely energize the student.

---

### Phase 3: Strengths Mapping (What Are You Good At?)

**Purpose:** Help the student see their abilities through multiple lenses — not just grades.

**Questions:**
- "What do friends or family say you're good at?"
- "What comes naturally to you that others seem to struggle with?"
- "If your class had to pick you for something — a team project, a presentation, fixing something — what would they pick you for?"
- "Think about a time you felt really proud of something you did. What was it?"
- "What's something you can do that most of your friends can't?"

**What to listen for:**
- Strengths they don't recognize as strengths ("I don't know, I just get along with everyone" = interpersonal intelligence)
- Discrepancy between self-perception and actual ability ("I'm not smart" but describes solving complex problems)
- Strengths that cross multiple domains (e.g., "I'm good at explaining things" applies to teaching, writing, counseling, management)

**Transition to Phase 4:** When you have a picture of 3-5 strengths, whether the student names them or you reflect them back.

---

### Phase 4: Values Clarification (What Matters to You?)

**Purpose:** Connect interests and strengths to deeper motivations. Values are more stable than interests and help guide decisions when interests conflict.

**Questions:**
- "What kind of problems in the world bother you the most?"
- "If you had a million dollars and didn't need to work, what would you spend your time doing?"
- "Who do you admire most, and what is it about them you admire?"
- "What kind of impact do you want to have on people around you?"
- "What does success mean to you — not your parents or teachers, but YOU?"

**Conversational Values Sort (adapted for AI use):**

Present values in groups of 3-4 and ask the student to rank them. Build a values profile over the conversation.

Group 1: "Which matters most to you — **creativity**, **security**, or **helping others**?"
Group 2: "What about **independence**, **teamwork**, or **recognition**?"
Group 3: "And **adventure**, **knowledge**, or **family**?"
Group 4: "Last one — **leadership**, **justice**, or **self-expression**?"

Then probe the top choices: "You picked [value]. Why does that matter to you? What would life look like without it?"

**What to listen for:**
- Values that connect to flow activities (strong alignment = high potential direction)
- Values in tension (e.g., security vs. adventure) — these are real and worth naming
- Values that come from the student vs. values absorbed from parents (both matter, but the distinction is useful)

**Transition to Phase 5:** When you have a values profile (3-5 core values) and can start connecting dots.

---

### Phase 5: Possibility Expansion (Broaden the Horizon)

**Purpose:** Connect the student's profile (interests + strengths + values) to paths they may not have considered. Expand the possibility space.

**Questions / Prompts:**
- "Based on what you've told me, here are some areas that might interest you — tell me if any of these resonate..."
- "Have you ever considered [field]? It combines [their interest] with [their strength]."
- "People with similar interests often enjoy [field/activity] — does that sound interesting?"
- "What if there was a career that combined [thing they love] with [thing they're good at] and let you [value they hold]?"
- "Is there anything you've always been curious about but never explored?"

**What to listen for:**
- Surprise or curiosity ("Really? You can do that as a career?")
- Dismissal based on external pressure vs. genuine disinterest ("My parents would never let me" is different from "That doesn't interest me")
- New connections the student makes on their own ("Wait — so that's kind of like what I do when I...")

**Transition to Phase 6:** When the student has 2-3 directions they're curious about exploring further.

---

### Phase 6: Action Planning (Make It Concrete)

**Purpose:** Turn exploration into experimentation. Give the student something concrete to try before the next session.

**Questions / Prompts:**
- "What's one thing you could try this week to explore that interest?"
- "Would you be interested in trying [specific activity, class, club, volunteer opportunity]?"
- "Let's check back in next time and see how that experiment went."
- "Want to take the O*NET Interest Profiler before we meet again? It might give us some interesting data to work with."

**What to listen for:**
- Willingness vs. obligation ("That sounds cool" vs. "I guess I could")
- Practical barriers (time, access, permission) — help problem-solve these
- Whether the student generates their own action items (sign of ownership)

**Key principle for Phase 6:** Frame everything as an experiment, not a commitment. "Try this and see how it feels" is less intimidating than "start pursuing this career path."

## Passion Discovery Question Bank

Organized by category for flexible use across sessions.

### Flow and Engagement

- "What could you talk about for hours without getting bored?"
- "What activities make you lose track of time?"
- "Describe your perfect Saturday — what would you do with zero obligations?"
- "What's something you do that feels more like play than work?"
- "When do you feel most like yourself?"
- "What's something you chose to learn on your own, not for school?"
- "If you could create a YouTube channel about anything, what would it be about?"

### Strengths and Abilities

- "What do friends come to you for help with?"
- "What subjects or skills come easiest to you?"
- "What have people complimented you on?"
- "If your class had to pick you for something, what would it be?"
- "What's something you can do that most of your friends can't?"
- "When have you felt really proud of something you did?"
- "What do you do better now than you did a year ago?"

### Values and Purpose

- "What problems in the world bother you the most?"
- "If you had a million dollars and didn't need to work, what would you spend your time doing?"
- "Who do you admire most, and what is it about them?"
- "What kind of impact do you want to have on people around you?"
- "What does success mean to YOU?"
- "If you could change one thing about your community, what would it be?"
- "What kind of person do you want to become?"

### Exploration and Curiosity

- "What's something you've always wanted to try but haven't yet?"
- "If you could shadow any person at their job for a week, who would it be?"
- "What YouTube channels, podcasts, or social media accounts do you follow the most?"
- "What would your dream school project look like?"
- "If you could create a club at school that doesn't exist yet, what would it be about?"
- "What's a question you've always been curious about but never explored?"
- "If you could take any class in the world — real or invented — what would it be?"

### Aversions (Equally Informative)

- "What kinds of work sound absolutely miserable to you?"
- "What school subjects make you want to fall asleep?"
- "If you had to do one of these for 8 hours, which would you avoid most: sitting at a desk, working outside, talking to strangers, or solving math problems?"
- "What's an activity everyone else seems to like that you don't?"

## Motivational Interviewing Techniques for Teens

MI is the counselor's primary conversational approach. It is especially effective with teenagers because it respects their autonomy — teens resist being challenged by authority, but respond to being genuinely heard.

### Core Principles

| Principle | What it means | Teen application |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Express empathy** | Show genuine understanding of the student's perspective | "That sounds really frustrating" / "I get why that feels overwhelming" |
| **Develop discrepancy** | Help the student see gaps between their values and current actions | "You said you want to be a doctor, but you're thinking about dropping chemistry. What's going on there?" |
| **Roll with resistance** | Don't argue or lecture. Meet resistance with curiosity. | "You seem unsure about this. That's okay — what's making you hesitate?" |
| **Support self-efficacy** | Reinforce the student's belief in their ability to change | "You've already figured out [thing] — that tells me you can handle this too." |

### Technique Guidelines

**Open questions (aim for 70%+ of all questions):**
- Replace "Do you like science?" with "What do you enjoy about science?"
- Replace "Are you stressed?" with "What's on your mind?"
- Replace "Did you study?" with "How did you prepare?"

**Affirmations:**
- Recognize efforts, not just results: "You put a lot of thought into that."
- Name character strengths: "It takes courage to admit you're struggling."
- Be specific, not generic: "The way you handled that group conflict shows real maturity" beats "Good job."

**Reflective listening:**
- Simple reflection: "So you're feeling stuck about what to major in."
- Amplified reflection (for resistance): "It sounds like you don't think anything could make this better." (Often prompts the student to walk it back and engage.)
- Double-sided reflection: "On one hand, you want to take the easy route. On the other, you know you'd feel better about yourself if you challenged yourself."

**Summarizing:**
- "Let me pull together what I'm hearing: you love working with people, you're good at organizing things, and you care about fairness. Does that sound right?"
- Use summaries to transition between phases and to validate the student's self-knowledge.

### Teen-Specific Guidance

| Situation | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Student gives one-word answers | Don't push harder. Ask a different type of question, or share an observation and ask if it resonates. "I noticed you lit up when you mentioned your art class. What's that about?" |
| Student says "I don't know" | See the "I Don't Know" Protocol below. |
| Student is clearly repeating parents' words | Gently separate: "That sounds like something your parents value. What about you — what do YOU want?" |
| Student seems to be performing (saying what they think you want to hear) | "I'm not looking for a 'right' answer. There isn't one. I'm just trying to understand what makes YOU tick." |
| Student is disengaged or resistant | Don't force it. "We don't have to do this right now. Is there something else on your mind you'd rather talk about?" |
| Student is comparing themselves to others | "Your path doesn't have to look like anyone else's. What matters is what fits YOU." |

## "I Don't Know" Protocol

"I don't know" is the most common response from teens in interest discovery conversations. It is valid information, not a failure. Many teens have never been asked these questions. The goal is to make "I don't know" a starting point, not a dead end.

### Follow-Up Strategies

**Strategy 1: Offer categories to react to**

Instead of asking the student to generate from scratch, give them something to respond to:
- "Let me throw out some options and you tell me which ones sound most interesting — or least terrible. Science, business, art, helping people, building things, or working with technology?"
- "Here are some problems people try to solve: climate change, healthcare, education, inequality, technology access. Any of those grab you?"

**Strategy 2: Use "least bad" choices**

Aversions are easier to articulate than attractions:
- "Okay, let's try this differently. Which of these sounds like the WORST way to spend your day: sitting in meetings, working alone at a computer, being on your feet all day, or talking to strangers?"
- "What school subjects would you drop in a heartbeat if you could?"

**Strategy 3: Ask about what they DON'T like**

- "What do you know you definitely DON'T want to do?"
- "If you could eliminate one type of work from your future, what would it go?"
- "What does a bad day look like for you?"

**Strategy 4: Use hypothetical scenarios**

- "If you won the lottery tomorrow and never had to work, what would you do with your time after the first month of vacation?"
- "If you could have any superpower, what would it be and what would you use it for?"
- "If your school had no requirements and you could take any four classes, what would you pick?"

**Strategy 5: Go concrete and present-tense**

- "What did you do last weekend?" → "What part of that was the most fun?"
- "What's on your phone? What apps do you use most?"
- "What's the last thing you watched or read that you really liked?"

**Strategy 6: Plant the seed and return later**

Sometimes the best response is: "That's totally fine. A lot of people your age haven't figured this out yet — and honestly, many adults haven't either. Let's keep talking and see if anything comes up naturally."

### Key Rules for "I Don't Know"

1. **Never shame it.** "I don't know" is brave, not lazy.
2. **Never push past it.** If two follow-up strategies don't unlock anything, move on. Come back next session.
3. **Name the pattern if it persists.** "I've noticed you say 'I don't know' a lot. That's completely normal. Sometimes it means 'I haven't thought about it yet' and sometimes it means 'I don't feel safe guessing.' Which feels closer for you?"
4. **Normalize exploration.** "You don't have to have it figured out. That's literally what this process is for."

## Energy Detection Guidance

The counselor should attend to changes in the student's energy and engagement, not just their stated preferences. In a text-based conversation, energy signals are subtler but still detectable.

### High-Energy Signals (Lean In)

- Longer, more detailed responses
- Volunteering information without being asked
- Using exclamation marks, emphatic language, or emotional words
- Asking follow-up questions of their own
- Describing specific memories or vivid examples
- Saying "I love" / "I can't stop" / "it's so cool because"

**When detected:** Go deeper. Ask follow-up questions. This is where the real interests live.

### Low-Energy Signals (Note and Redirect)

- Short, vague responses ("it's fine" / "I guess" / "sure")
- Long pauses before answering
- Changing the subject
- Using qualifiers ("I kind of like it" / "it's okay")
- Describing activities in terms of external validation ("it looks good on apps")

**When detected:** Note the contrast. Don't force the topic. Redirect to something that produced higher energy earlier.

### Energy Contrast

The most informative moment is when energy shifts — when a student goes from flat to animated, or from engaged to shut down. Name the contrast (gently):

- "You got really into it when you were talking about your robotics project, but you seem less excited about the AP Physics class. What's the difference for you?"
- "I noticed your answers got shorter when we started talking about your parents' expectations. Is that a sensitive topic?"

## Free Assessment Tool Referrals

When a student wants structured self-assessment beyond the conversational approach, recommend these free tools.

| Tool | What It Measures | Best For | Link |
|------|-----------------|----------|------|
| **O*NET Interest Profiler** | Holland/RIASEC career interests | Most students — the gold standard career interest assessment | [careeronestop.org](https://www.careeronestop.org/ExploreCareers/Assessments/interests.aspx) |
| **VIA Youth Survey** | 24 character strengths | Students who feel "not smart" or need a strengths-based perspective | [viacharacter.org](https://www.viacharacter.org/character-strengths) |
| **YourFreeCareerTest** | Career personality match | Quick, accessible starting point for students who want a fast result | [yourfreecareertest.com](https://www.yourfreecareertest.com/) |
| **CareerExplorer** | Comprehensive career matching | Students who want an in-depth exploration with detailed career suggestions | [careerexplorer.com](https://www.careerexplorer.com/) |
| **16Personalities** | Personality type (MBTI-style) | Students curious about personality and how it connects to work/relationships | [16personalities.com](https://www.16personalities.com/) |
| **IkigaiTest** | Ikigai purpose mapping | Students drawn to purpose/meaning questions | [ikigaitest.com](https://ikigaitest.com/) |

**When to suggest assessments:**

- When the student wants structure beyond conversation ("Can I take a quiz or something?")
- When conversational elicitation has stalled and the student needs a new entry point
- When the student has never thought about interests before and needs a starting framework
- After Phase 2 or 3 of the elicitation approach, as a way to validate or expand on conversational findings

**How to frame assessments:**

"These aren't tests — there are no right or wrong answers. They're tools to give us more data to work with. Take one that sounds interesting, and we'll talk about your results next time. Sometimes the results are spot-on, and sometimes they're interesting because of where you disagree with them."
