# admissions-knowledge — Admissions Process, Testing, and Financial Aid Knowledge Base

This module provides the counselor's knowledge base for the application process, standardized testing, school selection, essay strategy, and financial aid. Loaded by `testing`, `schools`, `essays`, `apply`, `financial`, and `summer`.

## Application Platforms

### Common App

The most widely used platform — 1,000+ member schools.

- Apply to up to 20 schools with one base application
- One personal essay (650 words max, 250 min) + school-specific supplements
- Seven essay prompts to choose from (unchanged for 2025-2026)
- Activity section: list up to 10 activities with 150-character descriptions
- **Default recommendation**: most students should use Common App for broadest reach

### Coalition App

~170 schools, focused on access and affordability.

- Free for students
- Virtual "locker" to store materials starting in 9th grade
- Five essay prompts, recommended 500-550 words (no hard limit)
- Only accepts schools that demonstrate commitment to financial aid
- Supplemental materials submitted on each school's own site

### UC Application

For all University of California campuses.

- 4 "Personal Insight Questions" (350 words each) chosen from 8 prompts
- Submission window: **November only**
- No early admissions process
- One application covers all UC campuses
- No letters of recommendation required (with some exceptions)

### Other Platforms

- **ApplyTexas**: Texas public universities
- **School-specific portals**: MIT, Georgetown, and others have their own applications
- **SCOIR/Naviance**: Many high schools use these for document management

**Guidance**: Check each target school's accepted platforms. Most students should start with Common App and add others as needed.

## Application Types

| Type | Binding? | Deadline | Decision By | Key Rule |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Early Decision (ED)** | Yes, binding | Nov 1-15 | Mid-December | Must attend if accepted; withdraw all other apps |
| **Early Decision II (ED2)** | Yes, binding | Jan 1-15 | Mid-February | Second chance at binding early commitment |
| **Early Action (EA)** | No | Nov 1-15 | January-February | Non-binding early notification |
| **Restrictive Early Action (REA)** | No, but restricted | Nov 1 | Mid-December | Can't apply EA/ED elsewhere (Harvard, Stanford, Yale, Princeton) |
| **Regular Decision (RD)** | No | Jan 1-15 | Late March-April | Standard timeline, most flexibility |
| **Rolling Admissions** | No | Varies | Ongoing | Apply early for best chances |

### Strategic Implications

**Early Decision (ED)**:
- ED acceptance rates are often significantly higher than RD
- Only recommend if: the school is absolutely the student's #1 choice, they have thoroughly researched and visited, and financial aid is not a deciding factor (cannot compare offers)
- **Financial risk**: aid offer arrives simultaneously with admission, but the student cannot compare packages across schools
- If the family needs to compare financial aid offers, ED is usually not the right strategy

**Early Action (EA)**:
- Get early answers without commitment
- Can apply to multiple EA schools simultaneously (except REA schools)
- Good for students who want options and time to compare aid packages
- Strategic advantage: reduces stress by securing acceptances early

**Restrictive Early Action (REA)**:
- Can apply REA to one school + public university EA programs
- Cannot combine with ED or private EA at other schools
- Offered by Harvard, Stanford, Yale, Princeton, and a few others

**Regular Decision (RD)**:
- Maximum flexibility to compare offers
- Best when: student needs to compare financial aid packages, is still strengthening their application (test scores, senior fall grades), or wants maximum options

**Rolling Admissions**:
- Apply as early as possible — spots fill as decisions are made
- Common at many large state universities

## Holistic Review

### What Schools Evaluate

Admissions officers at selective schools rate applicants across multiple dimensions. Schools like the Ivies use a 1-6 scale (1 = best):

| Component | Weight | What They Look For |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Academics** | Highest | Course rigor relative to what's available, GPA trend, class rank |
| **Extracurriculars** | Very High | Depth, impact, leadership, commitment (NOT quantity) |
| **Personal Qualities** | Very High | Character, resilience, intellectual curiosity, contribution potential |
| **Essays** | High | Authentic voice, self-awareness, writing ability, unique perspective |
| **Recommendations** | High | Teacher/counselor insights on character, classroom contribution |
| **Interview** | Moderate | Personality, communication, genuine interest |
| **Test Scores** | Moderate-High | SAT/ACT as baseline; AP scores show mastery |

### The "Relative to What Was Available" Principle

Admissions officers care about how you challenged yourself given your school's offerings. A student taking 5 APs at a school offering 20 is viewed differently than a student taking 5 at a school offering 5. This applies to:

- Course rigor (APs, honors, dual enrollment)
- Extracurricular opportunities (rural vs. urban, well-funded vs. under-resourced)
- Testing access (some students have more prep resources than others)

The counselor should help students maximize challenge within their actual context, not measure against an abstract standard.

## Demonstrated Interest

### Who Tracks It

~16% of schools rate demonstrated interest as "considerably important," and 28% rate it "moderately important."

| Cares Significantly | Generally Doesn't Care |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| Many liberal arts colleges | Ivy League schools |
| Mid-size private universities | Large public universities |
| Schools with lower yield rates | Schools with very high yield |

### What They Track

Schools quietly monitor:
- Email opens and click-throughs from admissions mailings
- Campus visits (virtual and in-person) — always sign in
- College fair sign-ins
- Webinar and event attendance
- Applicant portal login speed after acceptance
- Social media follows and engagement
- Interview participation (if optional, always do it)

### Actions to Take

1. Open emails from admissions offices
2. Attend virtual info sessions and webinars
3. Visit campus if possible (sign in at admissions)
4. Accept interviews — always, even if listed as "optional"
5. Write strong "Why Us" essays with specific details
6. Connect with admissions reps at college fairs
7. Follow up after visits with a brief thank-you email
8. Apply early (EA/ED signals strong interest)

**Guidance**: Focus demonstrated interest efforts on schools that track it (mostly LACs and mid-size privates). Don't waste energy on schools that don't track it (Ivies, large publics).

## College List Building

### Structure

- **Reach (2-3 schools)**: Acceptance is uncertain based on the student's profile — aspirational but not impossible
- **Match (4-5 schools)**: Profile aligns well with admitted student data — reasonable expectation of acceptance
- **Safety (2-3 schools)**: High confidence of acceptance AND the student would genuinely attend — a safety must be a school the student likes

### Evaluating Fit

For each school on the list, assess:

- **Academic fit**: Does the student's GPA and test score range fall within or near the school's middle 50%? Is the student's intended program strong at this school?
- **Activity/spike fit**: Does the school value the student's areas of depth? Are there clubs, research opportunities, or programs that match?
- **Cultural fit**: Size, location, campus vibe, social scene, diversity, Greek life presence
- **Financial fit**: Net price after aid, merit scholarship availability, need-based aid generosity

### Common List-Building Mistakes

- All reaches, no safeties (or safeties the student would never attend)
- Choosing schools by name recognition alone without researching fit
- Ignoring financial reality until after acceptance
- Not diversifying by school type (all large universities, or all LACs)
- Not considering location, size, and culture preferences seriously

## Testing Landscape (2025-2026)

### The Big Shift: Tests Are Back

Multiple top schools have reinstated SAT/ACT requirements for 2025-2026 admissions:
- **Reinstated**: Harvard, Yale, Brown, Dartmouth, Cornell, Stanford, Caltech, UT Austin, UPenn, U of Miami
- **Still test-optional**: Columbia, Princeton (as of 2026)
- **Key takeaway**: Even test-optional schools' data shows that submitting scores helps. Plan to test.

### SAT vs. ACT Comparison

| Feature | SAT | ACT |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Format** | Digital only, adaptive | Paper or online, linear |
| **Duration** | 2 hours 14 min | 2 hours 55 min (with writing) |
| **Sections** | Reading/Writing + Math | English, Math, Reading, Science (+optional Writing) |
| **Math** | Calculator always available, formulas provided, ~1:35/question | No formulas provided, ~1:07/question, more topics (matrices, logs) |
| **Science** | Integrated into reading/math | Separate science section |
| **Scoring** | 400-1600 | 1-36 composite |
| **Best for** | Deliberate thinkers, strong math reasoning | Fast readers, science-strong students, straightforward question preference |

**Recommendation**: Take a practice test of each by sophomore spring. Go with whichever feels more natural. Neither is inherently harder — different students suit different tests.

### Test-Optional Nuance

Even at test-optional schools, submitting strong scores helps. Data consistently shows that applicants who submit scores are admitted at higher rates than non-submitters. The practical guidance:
- If your score is at or above a school's 25th percentile, submit it
- If your score is below the 25th percentile, going test-optional may be strategic
- Applying to test-required schools means you must prepare regardless

### PSAT and National Merit

- PSAT taken sophomore fall is practice; PSAT taken junior fall qualifies for National Merit
- National Merit Semifinalist cutoffs vary by state (typically 1400-1520 PSAT range)
- National Merit status unlocks significant scholarship opportunities at many schools
- Some schools offer full-ride scholarships to National Merit Finalists

### Testing Timeline

| When | What |
|------|------|
| **Sophomore Fall** | Take PSAT (practice + National Merit qualifier) |
| **Sophomore Spring** | Take one practice SAT and one practice ACT to compare |
| **Junior Fall** | Begin targeted prep for chosen test |
| **Junior Spring** | First official SAT or ACT (March-June) |
| **Junior Summer** | Retake prep if needed |
| **Senior Fall** | Final retake if needed (most colleges accept October/November scores) |

## Essay Strategy

Detailed essay coaching happens in the `essays` command. This section provides the knowledge base the counselor draws from.

### Personal Statement Principles

- **Depth of reflection, not drama of story**: Admissions officers care about what the experience reveals about the student, not how dramatic it was
- **Show, don't tell**: Instead of "I'm determined," describe the moment that proves it
- **Go small to go deep**: A mundane moment explored deeply outperforms a dramatic event skimmed over
- **The "so what" test**: Every paragraph should answer "why does this matter?"
- **Find your voice**: Write like you talk — polished, but natural
- **Connect to growth**: What changed in how you think, see the world, or act?

### Supplemental Essay Types

- **"Why This School?"**: Be hyper-specific. Name professors, courses, programs, traditions. Show real research. Connect your interests to specific offerings. Explain what you'll contribute, not just what you'll get.
- **"Why This Major?"**: Tell the story of how your interest developed. Connect to experiences (classes, projects, mentors). Show you understand what the major involves. Connect to future goals.
- **"Community/Diversity"**: Define community broadly. Show how you've contributed and been shaped by it. Be genuine, not performative.
- **"Activity Elaboration"**: Deep dive on one activity. Focus on impact and growth, not description.

### Common Pitfalls

- **The resume essay**: Listing accomplishments the admissions reader already has in the activity section
- **Trauma without reflection**: Describing a difficult experience without showing growth or insight
- **Trying to sound smart**: Using SAT words and complex sentences instead of authentic voice
- **Generic statements**: "I've always loved science" or "Your diverse community" — could apply to anyone
- **Mentioning specific colleges in the Common App personal essay** (it goes to all schools)

### Narrative/Spike Model in Essays

The personal statement + supplementals + activity descriptions should tell a cohesive story that threads through:
- Course selection (aligned APs)
- Extracurricular activities (depth in 2-3 related areas)
- Summer experiences (programs/internships in the field)
- Essays (connecting the dots, explaining the "why")
- Recommendation letters (teachers who've seen the passion)

## Summer Programs

### Tier 1: Most Prestigious/Selective (Free)

These are programs admissions officers recognize by name:

| Program | Focus | Duration | Acceptance Rate |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| RSI (Research Science Institute) | STEM research at MIT | 6 weeks | ~2-3% |
| SSP (Summer Science Program) | Astrophysics, biochem, genomics | 39 days | ~7% |
| MITES Summer (MIT) | STEM + entrepreneurship | 6 weeks | ~8% |
| Simons Summer Research | Research at Stony Brook | 8 weeks | ~5% |
| Clark Scholars (Texas Tech) | Intensive research | 7 weeks | ~3% |
| Stanford SIMR | Medical research | 8 weeks | ~5% |
| COSMOS (UC system) | STEM clusters | 4 weeks | ~15% |

All Tier 1 programs are free or sliding scale. Most include stipends.

### Tier 2: Highly Regarded (Paid or Selective)

| Program | Focus | Cost | Notes |
|---------|-------|------|-------|
| LaunchX | Entrepreneurship | ~$5,000 | ~10% acceptance; build real startups |
| Yale Young Global Scholars | Multidisciplinary | ~$6,500 | STEM, humanities, social sciences |
| MIT Beaver Works (BWSI) | Applied engineering | Free | Autonomous vehicles, cybersecurity |
| Stanford SUMaC | Advanced math | ~$8,000 | Abstract algebra, number theory |
| Interlochen | Arts | ~$4,000-7,000 | Music, visual arts, theater, film |
| Bank of America Student Leaders | Leadership + nonprofit | Free | Community-focused |

### Tier 3: University Pre-College Programs (Paid)

These provide campus experience but are **less impressive for admissions** — they are essentially pay-to-attend programs:

| Program | Cost | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Harvard Pre-College | ~$6,100 | Good exposure; does NOT help Harvard admission |
| Stanford Pre-Collegiate Studies | ~$7,000-8,000 | Academic enrichment |
| Johns Hopkins Pre-College | Varies | Medicine, STEM, humanities |

**Important**: Admissions officers know the difference between selective programs (Tier 1-2) and pay-to-attend programs (Tier 3). Tier 3 programs are fine for exploration but don't boost applications the way Tier 1-2 do.

### Self-Directed Alternatives

If a student can't afford or get into formal programs, self-directed alternatives can be equally impressive:
- Start a meaningful project (blog, nonprofit, business, app)
- Cold-email professors at local universities for research opportunities
- Get a real job (shows maturity and responsibility)
- Deep independent study with a tangible output
- Volunteer for an organization you genuinely care about
- Create something: film, website, art series, community event

## Recommendation Letters

### Who to Ask

- **Junior year teachers preferred** (most recent academic evaluation)
- **Core subject teachers** (math, English, science, history, foreign language)
- Prioritize teachers who know you as a **person**, not just a grade
- A teacher where you struggled then grew can write a stronger letter than one where you coasted to an A
- Choose teachers from **different subjects** to show range
- Align at least one recommender with your intended major/spike

### When to Ask

- **Spring of junior year** is ideal
- Give at least 1 month before the earliest deadline
- For ED/EA (Nov 1), ask by early October at the latest
- Ask **in person, privately** (not by email, not in a group)

### How to Help Your Recommender

Provide them with:
- A "brag sheet" or resume of activities, accomplishments, and goals
- Specific examples of memorable moments in their class
- Why you chose them (what you hope they can speak to)
- Your intended major and college list
- Clear deadlines

## Financial Aid

### FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid)

- **Opens**: October 1 each year
- **Deadline**: June 30 (but submit as soon as possible — some aid is first-come)
- **Determines**: Federal grants (Pell Grant), loans, work-study
- **Required by**: Virtually all schools for any financial aid
- **Cost**: Free
- **Calculates**: Student Aid Index (SAI), which replaced Expected Family Contribution (EFC)

### CSS Profile (College Board)

- **Used by**: ~200 mostly private schools for institutional aid
- **Opens**: October 1
- **Deadlines**: Vary by school (most January 1 - March 31)
- **Determines**: School's own grant/scholarship money
- **Considers**: More financial detail than FAFSA (home equity, business assets, non-custodial parent income)
- **Cost**: $25 first school, $16 each additional (fee waivers available)
- **Unlocks**: Access to $14+ billion in non-federal aid

### Need-Blind vs. Need-Aware Schools

| Term | What It Means | Examples |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Need-Blind** | Doesn't consider ability to pay when admitting | Harvard, MIT, Princeton, Yale, Amherst, Brown |
| **Need-Aware** | May consider financial need in borderline cases | Most private colleges |
| **Meets Full Need** | Covers 100% of demonstrated financial need | ~25 schools nationally |
| **No-Loan** | Meets need entirely with grants, no loans required | Harvard, Princeton, MIT, Brown, Stanford |

Only ~12 schools are need-blind AND meet full demonstrated need — these are the most financially generous schools in the country.

### Scholarship Strategy

**Merit Scholarships**:
- Based on academics, talent, or achievement (not financial need)
- Strong SAT/ACT scores are the most realistic way to increase merit aid
- Many state universities offer automatic merit scholarships at GPA/test score thresholds

**Local Scholarships**:
- Less competition than national scholarships
- Ask the school counselor for a list
- Check community organizations (Rotary, Lions Club, local businesses)
- Smaller amounts ($500-5,000) but they add up

**National Scholarships**:
- Search on Fastweb, College Board Scholarship Search, Bold.org
- More competitive but larger amounts
- QuestBridge (full rides for high-achieving, low-income students)
- National Merit Scholarships (based on PSAT score)
- Gates Scholarship, Coca-Cola Scholars, Dell Scholars

**Essay Scholarships**:
- Many merit scholarships require essays
- Reuse and adapt Common App essay themes
- Apply broadly — many have fewer applicants than expected

### Financial Aid Negotiation

Financial aid packages are not always final. Families can appeal:
- If financial circumstances have changed since filing (job loss, medical expenses, divorce)
- If another comparable school offered significantly more aid (provide the offer letter)
- Write a professional, specific appeal letter to the financial aid office
- Be factual and respectful, not demanding
- This works more often than families expect — it is a normal part of the process

| When | What |
|------|------|
| **Junior Year Spring** | Run net price calculators on target schools |
| **Summer** | Gather financial documents; understand SAI |
| **October 1** | FAFSA and CSS Profile open — submit as soon as possible |
| **October-March** | School-specific financial aid deadlines |
| **December-April** | Aid offers arrive with admission decisions |
| **April** | Compare offers; appeal if needed |
| **May 1** | Commit to school; accept financial aid package |
